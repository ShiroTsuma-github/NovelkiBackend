      The story is good. The author has balls of steel in the areas he's willing to approach his novel in. It has a lot of the stuff I like in it.

      The harem characters are good, and so's the humor.

      The plot seems to have pacing at a rate that's just too wild. Only about a week has passed and that's at over 250 chapters. So a lot is happening all at once and you might feel overwhelmed with the info you're getting.

      A lot of the "college" stuff feels like it was originally written as highscool stuff. For example, Larry tells the class the MC is off his game because his confession was rejected by a girl. Another is where Larry assumes the MC thinks he's with Emma "I was going to introduce her [Emma] to you on your birthday!" Everyone seems to know the mc's reputation as a model student including the faculty. But if the MC was a college student, he'd be fresh out of highschool at 18. So the first few months at college wouldn't give him much of a reputation and unless this was an extremely small town, you'd rarely find your highschool buds in the same classes, let alone at the same college.       I couldn't even get past the beginning of the first chapter, only getting to the point where he knocked on the door for the interview. The writing is clipped and disjointed, bouning from topic to topic like that squirrel from Over the Hedge. One moment, he's talking about his family issues without letting you properly absorb any of the subjects, and then the next, he's talking about demon lords and demon hunters. All of this is done with excruciatingly subpar exposition and horrendous grammar.

      "Celia, who was watching TV in the living room remained silent, didn't answer me." This is one example of the bad grammar, bad exposition, as well as poor sentence structure. To begin with, the only mention of the sister was at the beginning of a 5 sentence, rambling paragraph that attempted to shove all the family info into one spot with the result of not really explaining anything. The reader could be forgiven if they were confused as to who Celia was at first. Since her name was mentioned when he called out to her just before, it could have solved this by having "My sister" instead. Next, the second comma is in the wrong spot, with "and" missing as well. Finally, that sentence should not have been its own paragraph, instead closing off the previous one.

      Here's another example. "That day I never asked father to pick me up. But father insisted, he drove his car in bad weather and ended up with that fateful accident." This review is already too long, so I'll show instead of tell why it is bad by fixing it this time for a comparison.

      "I [hadn't] asked Father to pick me up [that night.] [He had instead insisted on doing so himself because of the stormy weather.] [On the way there he ended up hydroplaning, leading to a fatal car crash.]" My version fixes all the issues I outlined above. It corrects all the grammar and adds proper exposition that allows the reader to better picture a father driving through a storm, losing control of his car, and crashing.

      My final example involves a different aspect of the author's writing style that irked me. They appear to prefer writing sound effects or actions instead of describing them. This could be forgiven if they didn't seem to flip flop on it. Here's an example.

      "* Knock * * Knock * * Knock *

      I knocked on the door."

      I already don't like the comic book asterisk sound effects, but following it with a description of the action is exponentially worse. If you're going to describe it, why are you using the asterisks in the first place?

      I understand that this may sound like elitist nitpicking. If these examples were few and far between, I would agree with you. However, these were all within the first 30 seconds of starting chapter 1. There are so many mistakes so quickly that it is rather foreboding.

      The premise was intriguing. I'm sure, with some vigorous editing, it would be a good story. As it is now, however, I don't think it's worth the read. I truly wish the author luck in refining their craft.
